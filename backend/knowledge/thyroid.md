# Thyroid Markers

## Purpose

Thyroid tests measure hormones and signals related to the thyroid gland. The thyroid helps regulate metabolism, energy use, temperature sensitivity, heart rhythm, bowel patterns, skin and hair changes, and many other body functions. Common thyroid tests include TSH, free T4, free T3, total T4, total T3, and thyroid antibodies.

AYU explains thyroid markers for educational purposes only. Thyroid interpretation depends on symptoms, pregnancy status, age, medications, supplements such as biotin, pituitary health, recent illness, and prior thyroid history. AYU must not diagnose thyroid disease or recommend medication changes.

## TSH

TSH stands for thyroid-stimulating hormone. It is produced by the pituitary gland and signals the thyroid to make thyroid hormones. TSH is commonly used as a screening marker because it often changes when thyroid hormone levels are outside the body's usual needs.

A higher TSH can be associated with the body asking the thyroid for more hormone. A lower TSH can be associated with reduced signaling to the thyroid. These are general patterns, not diagnoses. TSH should be interpreted with free T4, clinical context, and sometimes repeat testing.

## Free T4

Free T4 is the unbound portion of thyroxine, one of the main hormones made by the thyroid. T4 can be converted into T3 in body tissues. Free T4 helps clinicians understand available thyroid hormone level.

If TSH and free T4 move in different directions, the pattern can provide useful context. For example, a high TSH with low free T4 has a different meaning than a low TSH with high free T4. AYU should explain what the markers measure without assigning a diagnosis.

## Free T3

Free T3 is the unbound portion of triiodothyronine, a more active thyroid hormone. Free T3 may be checked in selected situations, but it is not always part of routine screening. T3 levels can vary with illness, nutrition status, medications, and lab methods.

AYU should describe T3 as part of the thyroid hormone system. It should not overstate the meaning of a single free T3 result.

## Total T4 and Total T3

Total T4 and total T3 measure both bound and unbound hormone. These values can be affected by proteins that carry thyroid hormones in the blood. Pregnancy, estrogen therapy, liver conditions, and some medications can influence binding proteins.

Free hormone tests are often more directly related to available hormone, but total hormone values can still be useful in some clinical contexts. Interpretation belongs with a qualified healthcare professional.

## Thyroid Antibodies

Thyroid antibody tests may include thyroid peroxidase antibodies, thyroglobulin antibodies, and TSH receptor antibodies. Antibodies are immune proteins. Their presence can suggest an immune-related thyroid pattern, but the meaning depends on the antibody type, level, symptoms, and hormone results.

AYU can explain that antibodies provide immune-system context. It must not conclude that the user has a specific autoimmune condition.

## Common Interpretation Principles

Thyroid tests can be affected by biotin supplements, recent severe illness, pregnancy, age, steroid medicines, amiodarone, lithium, and other medications. Some clinicians recommend pausing biotin before thyroid testing, but AYU should not provide personalized instructions. Users should ask their clinician or lab for guidance.

TSH can change slowly over weeks. One mildly abnormal value may be repeated before decisions are made. Reference ranges vary by laboratory and population. Pregnancy-specific ranges may apply.

AYU should explain each marker in plain language, identify whether the value is outside the provided reference range, and encourage discussion with a healthcare professional. It should avoid diagnosis, medication advice, dose changes, supplement recommendations, and treatment targets.

## Safety Note

Urgent medical care may be needed for severe symptoms such as chest pain, fainting, severe confusion, very rapid heartbeat, severe weakness, or extreme agitation. Lab interpretation should not delay urgent care when serious symptoms are present.
